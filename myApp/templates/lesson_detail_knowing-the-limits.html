Movers are experts at moving. Knowing where that expertise ends protects your clients—and you.

{{ lesson.meta.duration|default:"10–15 mins" }} {{ lesson.meta.level|default:"All Agents" }} Safety & Compliance

What Movers Can Do (Inside Their Scope)

  • Furniture Handling: Disassemble and reassemble beds, tables, shelves, and standard furniture.
  • Packing & Wrapping: Box household items, protect fragile goods with blankets, bubble wrap, and shrink film.
  • Loading & Unloading: Safely lift and place items onto trucks using dollies, straps, and pads.
  • Transport: Drive moving trucks (with a CDL if vehicle weight requires it).
  • Basic Setup: Place items in rooms as directed, reassemble standard furniture, reconnect plug-in electronics (e.g., a TV into a power strip).

What Movers Cannot / Should Not Do

  1. 1) Plumbing Work

    • Disconnecting or reconnecting refrigerator water lines, washing machines, or dishwashers.
    • Risk: leaks, flooding, mold, $10k–$50k damages.
    • Only licensed plumbers should do this.

  2. 2) Electrical Work

    • Removing light fixtures, wiring chandeliers, hard-wiring ovens.
    • Risk: shocks, fire hazards, code violations.
    • Requires licensed electricians.

  3. 3) Gas Work

    • Disconnecting/reconnecting dryers, stoves, or grills.
    • Risk: leaks, explosions, carbon monoxide poisoning.
    • Must be handled by a certified gas technician.

  4. 4) Prohibited Items

    • Hazardous materials: propane, paint, chemicals, firearms, explosives.
    • Perishable or living items: plants, pets, food.
    • High-value items: cash, jewelry, sensitive documents.

  5. 5) Specialty Installations

    • Built-in appliances, custom cabinetry, mounted TVs.
    • These require installers or contractors.

Why These Limits Exist

  • Insurance Boundaries: Movers’ liability coverage only applies to items they are authorized to handle. If they step outside scope, insurance often denies claims.
  • Regulatory Oversight: U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) and state agencies regulate moving companies, restricting what they can legally move or service.
  • Training Gap: Movers are trained in logistics, not trades. Asking them to do more is like asking a doctor to fix your car.

Key Takeaway: Movers are experts at moving, not at licensed trades. Realtors who clearly explain these boundaries to clients prevent costly misunderstandings.
